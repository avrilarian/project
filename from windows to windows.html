from Windows to Windows
April 12, 2016
by DIVYA RAJENDRAN

“How content marketing can build brand trust and affinity using the principles of window shopping.”

 

Before online shopping, we walked up and down the High Street gawping longingly in shop windows. In French, ‘to go window shopping’ is translated as faire du lèche-vitrine, literally to go window-licking—the idea that this activity provides a taster of what shops have to offer.

 

A shop window can tell us a number of things. It can tell us what a particular shop has in stock, or at least what kind of thing they are selling; it in some way embodies the character or brand identity of a particular shop or product; and it also signals to us whether the shop is at the cheaper end or the higher end of the market.

 

DISCONNECTED SHOPPING EXPERIENCE

 

This is a stage that has been absent from early e-commerce user journeys, which have focused on search, find, add to basket and check-out—with some browsing functionality, but online shoppers usually know what they have in mind at the outset.

 

The discovery, or window-shopping element, has largely remained on the high street, where people browse bricks-and-mortar shops to find what they want, and then look online for the cheapest price. In more recent times, social media services such as Pinterest and Facebook, online search engines such as Google, and marketplaces such as Amazon and eBay have added to an ever more spaghetti-like journey of browsing and discovery.

 

This has led to an increasingly disconnected shopping experience that is not only worrying for high street stores, who lose out to online retailers, but for online retailers too, who risk getting caught out by a buying process that begins simply with the question, ‘Who is the cheapest?’

 

The answer to this problem would be to somehow bring the window-shopping stage to the world of online retail, in order to make the shopping experience more coherent and to connect shoppers with the retail brand once more. But this is easier said than done, and often the solutions tend to repeat mistakes by being just as convoluted.

 

WINDOW OF OPPORTUNITY

 

So how do we do it? Content marketing is one way—marketing that aims to acquire and retain customers by building brand reputation through valuable and relevant content.

 

It’s a method of marketing that has developed online, as traditional advertising methods are increasingly ignored by users who deftly skim through large quantities of information and pay less attention to the likes of banner ads and magazine advertisements.

 

In theory, content marketing can help to bring more of the retail journey into the online store in a few different ways. Firstly, it brings retail brands back into the picture by helping to build affinity and trust with the brand, through valuable information and communication. Much like a well-dressed store window, powerful, original content, optimised for search terms and well written, can augment your social media efforts, start conversations with clients and customers and allow you to showcase your expertise.

 

Being seen as trustworthy, authoritative and engaged can lead to a higher conversion rate, making the final purchase more likely to happen on the retailer’s site. And thirdly, this content can be shaped using search engine optimisation to bring in more shoppers via the likes of Google.

 

Unfortunately, content marketing is often implemented in a way that is just as fragmented as the user journey it attempts to solve, undermining the benefits of good content.

 

THE LONG GAME

 

It’s worth noting that content marketing is very much a long game. You can’t expect to throw up a few posts and expect a huge ROI, you have to adopt it because you care. A content team which is passionate about providing insight and entertainment to a customer base is going to be way more effective than a finance manager who is concerned with which keywords are going to have the most impact. You know your customers, you know their needs and passions, if you can harness that with regular communication, and it’s another huge string to your bow.

 

Whatever your brand, aim to produce the best online magazine you can for you particular niche or area of expertise. In fact, why not produce a physical magazine? It worked for Net-a-porter. As content marketing guru Seth Godin said in a recent interview: “When I think about how much money someone like Gillette spends [on advertising], the question is: Why doesn’t Gillette just build the most important online magazine for men, one that’s more important and more read than GQ or Esquire? It doesn’t because in a zero-marginal-cost world, it’s cheaper than ever for them to do that.” If you don’t have a team of writers, hire them. And just keep content relevant and regular.

 

With e-commerce platform giving a wide range of products, users generally find themselves confused, jumping from one website to another, saving products in various wish lists and sharing them on social networking sites to seek advice. In an attempt to cut the process, few entrepreneurs have come up with a solution which allow users to discover, collect and share best products under one platform.

 

Sweetcouch, Scrapehere and Clipr are some start-ups which are leading their ways into the Indian e-commerce market. Despite of having similar business model, these start-ups are having no probem in acquiring a good customer base. These websites – in an effort to make the shopping an easy experience – provide the option of buying the product right away in a single click whic takes the customer to the checkout page.

 

A unique and cool feature they share is the introduction of ‘Window Shopping’ in the online retail segment. The idea behind this feature is that while shopping online, especially in fashion and lifestyle products category, people selects a product by the way it looks, rather than by reading its detailed information. These start-ups, therefore allow users to browse through several retailers’ products with a big, catchy product picture, its price and seller’s name on the product image.

 

Sweetcouch, founded by Rajesh Chokhani in September 2012 is witnessing a click through rate of 25-37% on its ‘Buy Now’ button and has succeeded in sending about 20,000 shoppers to various retailers till date.

 

Clipr, Mumbai based start-up is showcasing products from around 20 e-tailers and enjoying a cumulative growth rate of around 10%. It has also been recently gone through India-Quotient’s accelerator IQ Bootcamp.

 

Based in Bangalore, Scrapehere was launched in March 2013 and has already acquired 750 registered users. The start-up has accounted approximately 20,000 page hits in the month of March.

 

Launched by young entrepreneurs, these start-ups are trying to be the ‘Indian Pinterest’ of online retail. May be the ideas are same, but these start-ups are trying to build their community and a strong presence in the Indian online retail market.
